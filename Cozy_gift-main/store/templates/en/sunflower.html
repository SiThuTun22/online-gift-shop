Sunflower Cultivation Period and Soil Types

English Name: Sunflower
Botanical Name: Helianthus annuus
Class: Magnoliopsida (dicotyledons)
The optimal planting period for sunflowers is September to October. If soil moisture is sufficient, they can be cultivated post-rice harvest, extending into November and December. Sunflowers thrive in a range of soil types, from nutrient-rich sandy loam to clay soils. The ideal soil pH level is between 6.0 and 7.5. In arid regions, they can be grown in water-retentive soils, such as clay-based soils, which help maintain adequate moisture levels.

Sunflower Fertilization

Sunflower plants have an extensive root system, enabling them to absorb significant amounts of water and nutrients. Both organic and chemical fertilizers should be applied. Before soil preparation, incorporate 5 to 10 cartloads of organic matter(such as cow dung, poultry manure, or compost) per acre. During soil preparation, apply 112 pounds of urea, 56 pounds of Triple Superphosphate (TSP), and 56 pounds of potash fertilizer per acre. During the budding stage, supplemental application of 56 pounds of urea per acre is required to support optimal growth and yield.

Sunflower Plant Care and Maintenance

During 3-week critical period from budding to full flowering, inadequate soil moisture can halt flower development and severely reduce yield. Irrigation is essential at two key stages: 75% budding stage and 75% flowering stage. Weed control is crucial, as weeds can reduce sunflower yields by over 50%. Ensure the field remains weed-free during early growth. Within 7 days of seedling emergence, use inter-row cultivators to suppress weeds between rows. For weeds near the plant base, manual weeding is recommended to avoid damaging the roots. Regular maintenance during these phases ensures optimal growth and maximizes harvest potential.

Sunflower Pest Control and Harvesting

Sunflower crops are vulnerable to damage from a variety of pests, including green stink bugs, leaf-rolling and leaf-attaching insects, aphids, whiteflies, cutworms, long-horned beetles, flower borers, birds, and rodents. To prevent seed-borne diseases, seeds should be treated with fungicides before sowing. For protection against sunflower leaf blight, apply a fungicide spray three times at two-week intervals when 50% of flowers have bloomed. Harvesting should begin when back of the flower heads turns yellow. After harvesting, sunflowers must be sun-dried for 2–3 days to ensure proper moisture reduction. Regular monitoring and integrated pest management are critical to minimize yield loss and maintain crop quality.
